IEEE 802.1Qav · Forwarding & queuing for time-sensitive streams

CBSCredit-based shaper.

TSN · smoothing by credit dynamics

A shaper that spaces transmissions out over time so reserved classes — audio/video, ADAS sensor streams, control loops — do not bunch into bursts that hurt their own latency or starve other traffic. The mechanism is a signed credit counter that rises while the queue is waiting, falls while it transmits, and gates transmission on the sign of the counter. Not a priority flag, not a drop-when-exceeded policer — a smoothing mechanism that trades unused time for future send authorisation.

01The problem CBS is built to solve

Consider a high-priority AVB stream on a bridge. Strict priority alone would let it transmit back-to-back whenever frames queue, producing bursts. A token bucket would police it — dropping or remarking frames that arrive faster than the rate. Neither behaviour is what a reserved class wants. What it wants is to spread its allowed transmissions across time, so consumers downstream see a smooth stream, and so lower-priority traffic gets gaps to use the link. CBS delivers exactly that.

The credit wallet — mental model

01 · Earn
While a frame is waiting in the queue, credit accumulates at idleSlope. The longer the wait, the more credit — up to a ceiling of hiCredit.
02 · Spend
Transmission is allowed when credit ≥ 0. During transmission, credit falls at sendSlope (always negative). The queue may go into debt.
03 · Repay
Once credit is below zero, further transmission is blocked. The queue waits — earning credit at idleSlope — until credit returns to ≥ 0. Only then can the next frame go.

This "spend, then repay" cycle is what separates CBS from a token bucket. A token bucket policer would have dropped a frame that arrived with insufficient tokens. CBS instead delays the frame, and the waiting period during which credit recovers is precisely the gap that spaces the class out over time.

02The four parameters

idleSlope
Rate at which credit accumulates while the queue is waiting — bits per second. This is the reserved bandwidth for the traffic class. Adding up the idleSlopes of all CBS queues on a port gives that port's total reserved bandwidth.
sendSlope
Rate at which credit is drawn down while transmission is in progress. Derived, not configured: sendSlope = idleSlope − portTxRate. Always negative. On a 1 Gbps port with 100 Mbps idleSlope, sendSlope is −900 Mbps.
hiCredit
Maximum positive credit the counter can reach. Bounds burst accumulation during idle periods. Typically maxInterferenceSize × (1 − idleSlope/portTxRate) — just enough credit to cover the largest frame that could block this queue.
loCredit
Minimum (most negative) credit the counter can reach. Bounds how deep into debt one transmission can push the queue. Typically maxFrameSize × sendSlope/portTxRate — the cost of transmitting one maximum-size frame.

04Three scenarios to try

Under-rate
Set input rate below idleSlope (default 100 Mbps). Every frame can transmit immediately — credit never goes deeply negative because enough idle time passes between frames for it to recover. The output is simply paced by the input.
At-rate
Input rate = idleSlope. The credit oscillates around zero. After each transmission drops credit to loCredit, the wait interval recovers it to exactly 0 by the time the next frame is ready. Link utilisation for this class hits exactly idleSlope.
Over-rate
Input rate above idleSlope. Queue fills up. Credit still hits loCredit on each transmission, but now the source produces frames faster than credit can recover. The output rate locks at idleSlope regardless of offered load — the extra frames queue up and eventually get dropped when the queue overflows. This is the shaping in action: output is capped at the reserved rate, and the spacing between transmissions is uniform.

05Worked dimensioning example

A front-facing automotive camera producing an H.265 stream at 25 Mbps average, with bursts up to 40 Mbps over a 10 ms window. The carrier is a 1 Gbps zonal link. Target: deliver the stream deterministically to the ADAS ECU.

idleSlope 40 Mbps
  rationale Size to peak, not average. This gives jitter headroom so a short burst doesn't deepen queue residency.
sendSlope 40 − 1000 = −960 Mbps
  rationale Derived. While transmitting, credit crashes at 960 Mbps — the link's full rate minus the reservation.
hiCredit ≈ 11,520 bits (1500 B × (1 − 40/1000) × 8)
  rationale Just enough to cover the largest frame that could delay this queue (one full 1500 B BE frame in progress).
loCredit ≈ −11,520 bits (1500 B × sendSlope/portRate × 8)
  rationale The depth one full-size frame drives credit to. Recovery time from loCredit is ≈ 288 μs.

The 40 Mbps reservation on a 1 Gbps link consumes 4% of the port's bandwidth for this camera. If four cameras sit on the same gateway, total CBS reservation is 160 Mbps — well under the 75% of port rate that 802.1Qav recommends as the upper bound on aggregate reservations. The remaining 840 Mbps is available for best-effort traffic via SP/WRR/DRR on other queues.

06Where CBS sits in the shaping family

Token bucket · RFC 2697/8

  • Unsigned tokens
  • Empty → packet dropped or marked
  • Policing — enforces rate by loss
  • At ingress, against a contract

CBS · 802.1Qav

  • Signed credit, can go negative
  • Negative → queue waits, no drops
  • Shaping — enforces rate by delay
  • At egress, per traffic class

ATS · 802.1Qcr

  • Per-frame eligibility time
  • Frame waits until eligible
  • Shaping — per-hop composable
  • Multi-hop deterministic networks

The clean way to remember the three: token bucket drops frames above a rate, CBS delays frames above a rate, ATS delays frames with a per-frame eligibility time that composes across hops. CBS works beautifully at one or two hops; over more hops its credit errors accumulate, which is exactly the gap ATS was chartered to fill. In a zonal automotive gateway with short paths, CBS is usually sufficient and demands less state than ATS. In an industrial backbone with five hops and no gPTP, ATS is the upgrade path.
