Chapter 01 · Traffic shaping fundamentals

The token bucket.

A policer that answers one question: may this packet pass now? Tokens drip into a bucket at a steady rate. Packets consume tokens on arrival. If the bucket runs dry, packets wait or are marked non-conforming. Simple to state, subtle in its behaviour.

How it works

Three parameters and one invariant govern everything. Keep these in your head and the rest of the algorithm falls out naturally.

r · rate
Tokens arrive at a fixed rate of r tokens per second. This is the average rate the flow is allowed to sustain over the long run.
b · depth
The bucket holds at most b tokens. Any tokens that would overflow a full bucket are discarded. This is the burst — the maximum number of back-to-back tokens available after an idle period.
s · cost
A packet of size s bytes needs s tokens to pass. If tokens are in units of bytes, the cost is the packet's length. If tokens are packets, the cost is 1.
test
On arrival: if tokens ≥ s, forward the packet and subtract s from the count. Otherwise the packet is non-conforming — dropped, delayed, or remarked depending on policy.

Try it

Watch the bucket fill at rate r. Drop packets by clicking send or switch to steady or bursty mode. Green flashes mean the packet passed; red means the bucket was too empty to cover it. Notice what happens when you let the bucket fill and then release a burst — the bucket empties quickly, and the next packets face the full waiting cost.

A worked example

Consider a flow rate-limited to r = 1 Mbps with a b = 8 KB bucket, token unit = 1 byte. Packets are 1000 bytes.

steady
Packets arrive at exactly 1 Mbps (125 packet/s). Tokens arrive at 125,000/s. Each packet costs 1000 tokens. The bucket hovers near full — every packet passes.
burst
After 64 ms of idle, the bucket is full (8000 tokens). A sudden burst arrives: 8 packets back-to-back consume 8000 tokens in a few hundred microseconds. All 8 pass. The 9th packet faces an empty bucket and waits ~8 ms for enough tokens to accumulate.
sustained
If the source tries to sustain 2 Mbps (twice r), the bucket drains faster than it fills. After the initial burst budget is spent, exactly half the packets fail — the long-term rate is clamped to r regardless of offered load.

Where it's used

Token bucket is the default policer in Linux tc, the shaping primitive behind committed information rate on carrier Ethernet, and — in a modified form — the basis of the Credit-Based Shaper used in TSN. The credit counter in CBS is conceptually a signed token bucket with different accumulation and drain slopes.
