IEEE 802.1Qcr-2020 · amendment to 802.1Q

ATSAsynchronous traffic shaping.

TSN · per-hop shaping, no global clock

A shaper that delivers zero-congestion-loss, bounded-latency forwarding without synchronising the network to a common transmission calendar. Every hop reshapes traffic using only its own local state. The mechanism that makes this work — and the idea worth understanding deeply — is the eligibility time stamped on each frame at arrival.

01What "asynchronous" means here

TSN has two shaping philosophies that most people conflate. 802.1Qbv TAS (Time-Aware Shaper) operates on a network-wide schedule: every bridge runs a repeating gate calendar synchronised to nanosecond precision via gPTP, and frames leave only when their gate is open. The guarantees come from the schedule itself. 802.1Qcr ATS takes the opposite approach: each hop computes, independently, when each arriving frame is allowed to leave. No shared calendar. No gPTP dependency for the shaping itself.

The practical consequence is large. ATS makes it possible to add deterministic shaping incrementally — you can upgrade one bridge at a time, mix ATS and non-ATS hops, and you don't need to rebuild your time-sync infrastructure to introduce bounded-latency streams. The trade-off is that ATS delivers slightly worse worst-case latency than a well-planned TAS schedule, because each hop introduces its own shaping delay instead of frames slotting into a pre-computed slot.

02Eligibility time — the central idea

When a frame arrives at an ATS scheduler, the scheduler computes a future time — the frame's eligibility time — at which the frame is allowed to be transmitted. Until that moment arrives, the frame waits in FIFO order. The eligibility time is derived from the committed rate, the committed burst size, the frame length, and the scheduler's local history.

CIR
Committed Information Rate — bits per second. The average rate the scheduler paces the flow to.
CBS
Committed Burst Size — bits. Short bursts up to this size may leave immediately; larger bursts are stretched in time.
MaxResidenceTime
Maximum time a frame may spend waiting in the shaper before it must be dropped. Bounds the queue to a finite delay.
bucketEmptyTime
Per-scheduler state: the time at which the virtual token bucket would next empty if no more frames arrived. Rolls forward by L × 8 / CIR every admitted frame.
groupEligibilityTime
Per-group state: the eligibility time of the most recently admitted frame in this scheduler group. Enforces non-decreasing eligibility across the group.

The eligibility-time computation

On frame arrival at time t with length L bytes, in simplified form:

bucketEmptyTime = max(bucketEmptyTime, groupEligibilityTime)
scheduleEligibility = bucketEmptyTime − (CBS − L) × 8 / CIR
lengthRecovery = L × 8 / CIR
// admission
if scheduleEligibility ≤ t + MaxResidenceTime:
  frame.eligibilityTime = max(t, scheduleEligibility)
  bucketEmptyTime += lengthRecovery
  groupEligibilityTime = frame.eligibilityTime
else:
  drop frame

Read the formula this way. The first line rolls the bucket forward to at least the group's current horizon — this is what makes the group's eligibility ordering non-decreasing. The second line computes when the virtual bucket will have enough tokens to cover this frame: it's bucketEmptyTime minus a constant offset determined by how much "prepaid" burst budget (CBS) the flow has. The third line charges the bucket for the frame's cost. If the computed eligibility is too far in the future to meet the residence bound, the frame is dropped — that's the hop's admission control.

The critical invariant: frames within a scheduler group receive non-decreasing eligibility times. That is why the shaper can be implemented as a FIFO — the frame at the head of the queue is always the one that becomes eligible first. No priority queue, no sorting, no per-frame timer scan.

05Why non-decreasing eligibility matters

The subtle beauty of ATS lies in a property that the standard emphasises but the introductions often skim past: frames within one scheduler group receive non-decreasing eligibility times. Formally, if frame A is admitted before frame B, then eligibility(A) ≤ eligibility(B).

This sounds trivial, but it has a large practical payoff: it means the shaper never needs a priority queue or a sorted heap. A simple FIFO is sufficient because the frame at the head of the FIFO is always the one that becomes eligible first. The scheduler's job at release time is reduced to "is the clock ≥ head's stamp? yes → dequeue and send; no → wait." This is implementable in hardware at line rate on the cheapest silicon.

The non-decreasing property is maintained by the bucketEmptyTime = max(bucketEmptyTime, groupEligibilityTime) line in the algorithm. Without that clamp, a frame arriving after a period of idle could end up with an eligibility time earlier than a frame already queued — and then FIFO order would be wrong and you'd need a heap. The clamp costs one comparison per arrival and saves you an entire data structure.

06Where ATS fits in your TSN toolkit

CBS · 802.1Qav

  • Single rate (idleSlope)
  • Credit-based state per queue
  • Latency bound grows with hops
  • Simplest; suits 1–2 hop designs
  • No global clock needed

TAS · 802.1Qbv

  • Gate calendar per port
  • Requires gPTP network-wide
  • Tightest latency guarantees
  • Schedule must be planned end-to-end
  • Brittle to topology change

ATS · 802.1Qcr

  • Per-frame eligibility time
  • Local state only; no global clock
  • Bounded latency without planning
  • Composes across many hops
  • Suits large, changing topologies

The mental model that puts the three shapers in a clean progression: CBS smooths a flow at one hop. TAS coordinates flows across the whole network on a shared timeline. ATS smooths flows independently at every hop, and the independence is the point. For a two-hop automotive gateway, CBS is usually sufficient. For a five-hop industrial backbone that must be bounded, TAS is the textbook answer but requires nanosecond-synchronous gPTP. For the same backbone when gPTP is impractical or the topology needs to flex, ATS is what 802.1Qcr exists to deliver.
