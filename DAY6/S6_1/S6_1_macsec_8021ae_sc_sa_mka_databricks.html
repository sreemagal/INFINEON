IEEE 802.1AE MACsec learning artifact

MACsec architecture through Secure Channels, Secure Associations, and MKA

MACsec protects Ethernet frames at Layer 2. The clean mental model is to separate the data plane, where a MAC Security Entity inserts a SecTAG and validates an Integrity Check Value, from the control plane, where MKA discovers authenticated peers and distributes Secure Association Keys.

1. Standards map

802.1AE and MKA cooperate, but they solve different problems.

IEEE 802.1AE

Defines MAC Security, the SecY, secured frame format, Secure Channels, Secure Associations, replay protection, cipher suites, validation, and management objects.

SecYSCSASecTAG

IEEE 802.1X MKA

Defines MACsec Key Agreement. MKA discovers mutually authenticated peers, elects a Key Server, distributes SAKs, advertises cipher capability, and maintains live peer state.

EAPOLMKPDUCAKCKNSAK

IEEE 802.1AEdk

Adds MAC privacy protection concepts that complement MACsec by reducing correlation from visible MAC addresses, frame sizes, and timing. It is adjacent context, not required for basic SC/SA/MKA learning.

privacyYANG/MIB
Standards-aware teaching rule: MACsec is not an end-to-end IP security protocol. It protects the Ethernet segment or Layer-2 service where the MACsec peers and their SecYs are established.

3. Object model: CA, SC, SA, SCI, AN, PN

These objects describe how a receiver interprets each protected frame.

SecY

The MAC Security Entity that applies and validates MACsec protection.

CA

Connectivity Association. The set of systems authorized to communicate securely, maintained by MKA using CAK and CKN.

SC

Secure Channel. A unidirectional protected stream from one transmitting SecY, normally identified by SCI.

SA

Secure Association. A keying instance within an SC. The two-bit AN selects the SA.

SCI

Secure Channel Identifier. It identifies the transmitting SC, commonly understood as a 48-bit MAC address plus a 16-bit port identifier. If carried in the SecTAG, the receiver can explicitly select the receive SC.

AN and PN

The Association Number selects the SA inside the SC. The Packet Number is monotonically advanced and used to reject replays, old frames, and frames outside the replay window.

VLAN placement caution

In a simple port-based deployment, outer DA/SA and the MACsec EtherType remain visible, while the original client EtherType and payload are protected. If service tags must remain visible to intermediate devices, the SecY placement must be designed accordingly.

Diagnostic hint: A switch cannot forward based on an encrypted customer VLAN unless it decrypts first or that tag was deliberately outside the protected client data.

5. MKA: key agreement for MACsec

MKA tells the SecY who is authorized and which SAK, AN, cipher suite, and replay settings to use.

In WAN MACsec, alternate EAPOL destination MAC or EtherType may be configured so provider bridges do not consume link-local EAPOL frames.

MKA state story

1. CAK and CKN exist.
Peers share or derive a Connectivity Association Key and identify it by Connectivity Association Key Name.
2. MKPDUs prove membership.
Participants exchange protected MKA state and advertise capabilities.
3. Key Server is elected.
One peer selects cipher suite and distributes SAKs.
4. SecY installs SAs.
Transmit and receive SAs become usable, so the controlled port can pass protected data.
5. Rekey.
New SAK is mapped to a new AN, while old receive SA remains briefly to avoid drops.

6. Interesting scenarios

Each example follows what the transmitter sends and what the receiver accepts or rejects.

A. Two ECUs establish MACsec

Normal bring-up using a pre-shared CAK.

MKASecYSAK installed
Initial condition: ECU-A and ECU-B know the same CKN and CAK. Controlled data is not yet passing.
MKPDU exchange: Both send EAPOL-MKA frames and prove membership in the same CA.
Key Server: ECU-A wins and distributes SAK0.
SA install: A transmits with AN=0. B installs receive SA for A's SCI and AN=0.
Data starts: B validates SCI, AN, ICV, and PN before releasing plaintext to the MAC client.
Key insight: MKA does not encrypt user data itself. It creates the SA state that lets the SecY protect user data.

B. Rekey without frame loss

Why old and new SAs overlap.

TimeTransmitterReceiverObservation
T0Uses SAK-old, AN=0.Accepts AN=0.PN rises under old SA.
T1MKA distributes SAK-new for AN=1.Installs AN=1 but keeps AN=0.Two ANs may be valid.
T2Switches transmit to AN=1.Accepts new frames.New PN context starts.
T3Stops using AN=0.Retires old receive SA.Late AN=0 frames now drop.
Not a bug: Two valid ANs during rollover can be correct and intentional.

C. Replay of captured frame

PN and replay-window behavior.

History: Receiver accepted SCI=A, AN=0 up to PN=10200.
Attack: Old frame with PN=9980 is injected.
Lookup may succeed: SCI and AN identify a valid SA.
Replay check fails: PN is stale or already seen, so the frame is discarded.
Ethernet FCS can be valid
MACsec ICV can even verify
Replay protection can still drop the frame because PN is too old

D. SCI absent

Why SCI presence matters.

If SCI is present, the receiver explicitly maps the frame to the transmitting Secure Channel. If SCI is absent, the receiver must infer the SC from local context such as the port and expected peer.

Safe: point-to-point link with only one expected peer.
Risky: multi-access or provider-style service with ambiguous transmitters.

F. WAN MACsec EAPOL trap

MKA never reaches the peer.

Standard EAPOL uses a link-local multicast destination. An intermediate provider bridge may consume or filter it instead of forwarding it through the Layer-2 service.

Site A sends DA=01-80-C2-00-00-03, EtherType=0x888E, MKPDU
Provider bridge consumes or filters it
Site B never receives MKA
Result: ordinary Ethernet service may work, but MACsec never comes up

G. 32-bit PN exhaustion

Why XPN exists.

A transmit SA must not reuse the same key and packet-number combination. On high-speed links with small frames, a 32-bit PN space can be consumed quickly. Rekey before exhaustion or use XPN-capable cipher suites.

32-bit PN space ≈ 4.29 billion packet numbers
High packet rate + long-lived SA = unsafe reuse risk
Safe response = rekey or use extended packet numbering

8. Header-reading drills

Use these as lab or classroom exercises.

Drill 1: Is this MACsec-protected?
DA      = 02-00-00-00-00-B2
SA      = 02-00-00-00-00-A1
Type    = 88-E5
TCI/AN  = 2C
SL      = 00
PN      = 00-00-10-5A
SCI     = 02-00-00-00-00-A1-00-01

Answer: Yes. EtherType 0x88E5 identifies MACsec. The receiver uses SCI to find the SC, AN to choose the SA, PN for replay checks, and ICV for validation.

Drill 2: Why does IP never see this frame?
Ethernet FCS valid
EtherType 0x88E5 present
SCI matches known peer
AN = 1
SecY counter: invalid ICV +1

Answer: MACsec validation failed before delivery to the controlled MAC service. Ethernet FCS success is not MACsec success.

Drill 3: Why does one direction fail after rekey?
A → B: A transmits AN=2, B still accepts only AN=1
B → A: B transmits AN=1, A accepts AN=1

Answer: SCs are unidirectional. A-to-B SA state can fail independently from B-to-A.

Drill 4: Why does WAN MACsec not come up?
Site A sends:
  DA = 01-80-C2-00-00-03
  Type = 88-8E
  Payload = EAPOL-MKA
Site B receives: nothing

Answer: A provider bridge may consume or filter link-local EAPOL frames. MKA cannot establish the CA.
