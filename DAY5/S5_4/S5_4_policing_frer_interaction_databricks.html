Policing + FRER Interaction

Policing and FRER do not merely coexist. Their ordering changes what gets counted, dropped, hidden, or recovered.

Per-Stream Filtering and Policing (PSFP) protects the bridge and downstream network from disruptive traffic. Frame Replication and Elimination for Reliability (FRER) protects the application from packet loss by sending redundant copies and eliminating duplicates. The subtle part is this: standards-model policing can occur after stream identification but before FRER recovery, so a frame later discarded as a duplicate may already have consumed gate, meter, and counter resources.

1. Core mental model

Think in two planes: the wire PDU and the bridge’s internal service metadata.

Wire PDU

The Ethernet frame carries MAC addresses, optional VLAN tags, optional R-TAG, EtherType/length, and payload. PSFP adds no protocol header of its own.

A packet can be policed without having a special “policing tag” on the wire.

Internal metadata

The switch pipeline attaches internal values such as stream_handle, priority, input port, sequence_number, gate state, color, and drop eligibility.

FRER and PSFP share the stream identification result.

Ordering

In the IEEE 802.1CB bridge model, flow metering is placed after passive stream identification and before individual or sequence recovery. That means PSFP can be charged for frames that FRER later discards.

This is the main interaction trap.

Standards-consistent takeaway

PSFP is not a substitute for FRER. PSFP decides whether a frame is allowed to continue based on stream, priority, size, time, and bandwidth contract. FRER decides whether an allowed frame is a useful first copy, an out-of-order useful copy, a duplicate, a rogue sequence, or a tagless case.

Engineering takeaway

A redundancy design must budget policing for the actual placement. If policing is applied to each member stream before elimination, the network sees and meters the redundant copies, not just the application’s unique payload rate.

4. PSFP: what policing sees and decides

PSFP is table-driven. It first selects a stream filter using stream and priority, then applies gate, filter, and meter behavior.

Stream filter

Matches stream_handle and priority. It points to a stream gate and optionally to a flow meter. It can also enforce maximum SDU size.

  • Match specific stream or wildcard stream.
  • Match specific priority or wildcard priority.
  • First matching ordered filter wins.
  • No match means ordinary processing unless a catch-all closed filter is configured.

Stream gate

Open gates pass frames. Closed gates drop frames. A gate may also assign an internal priority value and execute a time-aware control list.

  • Open or closed state.
  • Optional IPV for traffic class selection.
  • Optional interval octet limit.
  • Optional latch-on-invalid-rx behavior.

Flow meter

Bandwidth profile metering colors frames green, yellow, or red using committed and excess rate/burst parameters.

  • Green: within committed profile.
  • Yellow: excess but possibly still allowed.
  • Red: discard.
  • DropOnYellow controls whether yellow is discarded or only marked drop-eligible.

5. FRER: what recovery sees and decides

FRER recovery operates on the stream and sequence context after classification and sequence decode.

Sequence recovery classification

A sequence recovery function receives a stream context and a sequence number. It then decides whether the arrival is useful or should be eliminated.

300new
300duplicate
299old unseen
260rogue
—tagless
  • New: higher acceptable sequence, advance recovery state.
  • Duplicate: already accepted in the recovery history, discard.
  • Old but unseen: may pass if still inside the recovery window.
  • Rogue: too far outside the recovery window, discard.
  • Tagless: handled according to frerSeqRcvyTakeNoSequence.

Individual recovery versus sequence recovery

Individual recovery can be applied to a single member stream before merging. Sequence recovery eliminates duplicates across the compound stream.

Member stream A: seq 300, 301, 302... Member stream B: seq 300, 301, 302... Individual recovery can suppress repeated junk on A alone. Sequence recovery suppresses the second valid copy across A+B.
Why this matters with policing: A bad member stream can consume PSFP resources unless it is also gated, metered, or individually recovered in the right place.

6. Integrated switch pipeline

This is the most useful standards-consistent mental pipeline for the policing + FRER interaction.

1. Ingress parserRead MAC, VLAN, R-TAG, IP/UDP/TCP, payload markers.wire fields
2. Stream identificationAssign stream_handle using 802.1CB methods.stream_handle
3. Sequence decodeExtract sequence_number from R-TAG or other sequence encoding.sequence_number
4. Stream filterMatch stream_handle + priority; select gate and meter.PSFP
5. Gate + max SDUDrop if closed, oversize, or interval octet budget exceeded.PSFP
6. Flow meterGreen/yellow/red decision. Red drops; yellow may drop or mark.PSFP
7. Individual recoveryOptional per-member duplicate suppression and fault containment.FRER
8. Sequence recoveryEliminate duplicates across redundant copies.FRER
9. Queue / egressUse priority or IPV for traffic class and scheduling.802.1Q
Critical insight: In this model, PSFP can count and meter both redundant copies even though FRER will later forward only one useful copy to the listener.

Why standards place metering early

Policing is a network protection function. It prevents disruptive transmissions from consuming bridge resources and harming downstream traffic. If duplicate or rogue traffic were eliminated only later, the bridge might already have spent scarce bandwidth, gate budget, memory, or processing resources on it.

Why engineers get surprised

Reliability engineers often think in application payload rate after duplicate elimination. Switch policers often think in received frame rate before elimination. Those are different rates when redundancy is active.

9. Debugging and counter interpretation

FRER can hide faults from applications. PSFP counters tell you where frames were rejected before recovery.

Good commissioning checklist

  1. Verify stream identification before policing.
  2. Verify that each redundant member stream maps to the intended filter and meter.
  3. Size CIR/CBS/EIR/EBS for the rate actually seen by the meter.
  4. Check gate schedules on both redundant paths.
  5. Read FRER duplicate, rogue, tagless, passed, and reset counters together with PSFP counters.
  6. Test talker reset and sequence wrap-around explicitly.

Design rule of thumb

Do not configure PSFP from the application’s single-copy rate unless you know the policer is before replication or after duplicate elimination. If the policer sees redundant copies, budget for the member-stream aggregate that reaches that policer.
