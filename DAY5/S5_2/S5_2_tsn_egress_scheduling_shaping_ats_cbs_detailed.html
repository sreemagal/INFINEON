Ethernet switch egress scheduling and shaping with precise ATS and CBS calculations

This training note explains how an Ethernet switch egress port decides which frame leaves next, and how Time-Sensitive Networking mechanisms shape, gate, and arbitrate traffic. The emphasis is on implementer-level understanding: variables, formulas, state updates, numeric examples, and the difference between shaping and scheduling.

1. Start with the actual egress-port problem

A switched Ethernet egress port is a contention point. Many frames can be waiting in different traffic-class queues, but the physical link can serialize only one frame at a time. So a bridge port must answer three separate questions.

Gate

Which queues are allowed?

A time gate or other eligibility condition can make a queue unavailable even if it has frames.

Scheduler

Which allowed queue wins?

Strict Priority, Enhanced Transmission Selection, Weighted Round Robin, or Deficit Round Robin can decide among available queues.

Shaper

How is bursting controlled?

Credit-Based Shaper and Asynchronous Traffic Shaping control when traffic is allowed to participate, so it does not damage latency of other traffic.

Key idea: queue selection, shaping, and gating are not the same function. A real Time-Sensitive Networking design often layers them on the same egress port.

2. A switch-pipeline view

It is easier to understand CBS, ATS, TAS, ETS, WRR, and DRR if you place them in an egress pipeline rather than treating them as isolated buzzwords.

Ingress frame
   |

   |

The exact silicon architecture can vary, but the conceptual separation remains useful: ATS and CBS influence eligibility, while SP, ETS, WRR, or DRR arbitrate among eligible choices. TAS can completely suppress a queue by closing its transmission gate.

3. Strict Priority first, because it is the baseline

Strict Priority is the simplest useful transmission-selection rule. If several queues are eligible, the highest-priority non-empty eligible queue is selected first.

Priority 7 queue: non-empty and eligible  -> selected
Priority 6 queue: non-empty and eligible  -> waits
Priority 5 queue: non-empty and eligible  -> waits
Priority 0 queue: non-empty and eligible  -> waits

This gives excellent latency to the highest class. The danger is starvation: if a high-priority queue is continuously eligible, lower-priority queues may receive little or no service. This is why shaping, gating, and weighted allocation are added around strict priority.

4. Credit-Based Shaper from first principles

Credit-Based Shaper (CBS), introduced by IEEE 802.1Qav and now integrated into IEEE 802.1Q, shapes a traffic-class queue by maintaining a credit value. The queue can transmit only when its credit is non-negative and the queue also wins the normal scheduler arbitration.

What CBS is trying to prevent

Without CBS, a high-priority class can release a burst of back-to-back frames. Those frames can block best-effort or lower-priority traffic for a long time.

What CBS actually does

CBS lets the class consume a configured bandwidth share over time. It allows some bursting, but the burst is bounded by the credit limits.

CBS state variables and parameters

Symbol / parameterMeaningImplementation intuition
creditCurrent credit of the shaped traffic-class queue.If credit is negative, the queue has recently consumed more than its immediate allowance and must wait.
idleSlopeRate at which credit increases when the queue has a waiting frame but cannot transmit.The configured bandwidth reserved for this shaped class.
sendSlopeRate at which credit changes while the queue is transmitting. It is negative.The shaped class spends credit while it occupies the link.
hiCreditMaximum positive credit.Caps how much credit can be accumulated while the queue is blocked by interfering traffic.
loCreditMinimum negative credit.Bounds how deeply the queue can go into debt after transmitting a maximum frame.
RPort transmit rate.For example, 100 Mbit/s, 1 Gbit/s, or 2.5 Gbit/s.
Practical rule: CBS is queue-based. It shapes a traffic class or shaped queue, not every stream independently. Multiple streams mapped into the same CBS queue share that queue's credit behavior.

5. CBS formulas, credit evolution, and examples

5.1 Core slope formula

CBS send slope
sendSlope = idleSlope − portTransmitRate

Since idleSlope is normally smaller than the port transmit rate, sendSlope is negative. This is why credit decreases while the queue transmits.

5.2 Credit bounds

Maximum positive credit
hiCredit = maxInterferenceSize × idleSlope / portTransmitRate
Minimum negative credit
loCredit = maxFrameSize × sendSlope / portTransmitRate

These formulas are commonly shown in bytes when the frame sizes are in bytes. The same idea works in bits if every size is represented in bits.

5.3 CBS transmit eligibility

For a CBS-shaped queue to transmit its head-of-line frame:

1. The queue must contain a frame.
2. Its transmission gate, if TAS is also configured, must be open.
3. Its CBS credit must be greater than or equal to zero.
4. No higher-priority eligible queue may win the local scheduler arbitration.

This means CBS does not by itself say “transmit now.” It says “this shaped queue is allowed to compete now.”

5.4 Credit update rules in plain language

waiting
If the CBS queue has a frame waiting but cannot transmit because another queue/frame is using the port, credit increases at idleSlope, up to hiCredit.
sending
While the CBS queue is transmitting, credit decreases at sendSlope, down to loCredit.
negative
If credit is negative, the queue is not eligible. It must wait until credit recovers to zero.
empty
If there is no frame waiting, CBS does not let positive credit grow forever. Practical implementations reset idle positive credit to zero.

5.5 Worked CBS example

Assume a 100 Mbit/s egress port and one CBS-shaped traffic class reserved at 20 Mbit/s.

portTransmitRate R = 100 Mbit/s
idleSlope I       = 20 Mbit/s
sendSlope S       = I − R
                  = 20 − 100
                  = −80 Mbit/s

If the maximum interfering frame is 1542 bytes and the maximum shaped frame is also 1542 bytes:

hiCredit = 1542 × 20/100
         = 308.4 bytes

loCredit = 1542 × (−80/100)
         = −1233.6 bytes

Now consider a shaped 1000-byte frame. Its serialization time on the 100 Mbit/s link is:

frameTime = 1000 bytes × 8 / 100,000,000 bit/s
          = 80 µs

During those 80 µs of transmission, credit changes by:

creditChange = sendSlope × frameTime
             = −80,000,000 bit/s × 80 µs
             = −6400 bits
             = −800 bytes

After that frame, if credit is −800 bytes, the recovery time back to zero is:

recoveryTime = 800 bytes × 8 / 20,000,000 bit/s
             = 320 µs
Interpretation: this CBS queue cannot send another frame immediately if its credit is negative. CBS deliberately spaces the shaped traffic to maintain the configured long-term rate.

6. Asynchronous Traffic Shaping from first principles

Asynchronous Traffic Shaping (ATS), defined by IEEE 802.1Qcr, assigns an eligibility time to frames. A frame is not allowed to participate in transmission selection until the current time is greater than or equal to that eligibility time.

What makes ATS different from TAS?

ATS does not require a globally synchronized repeating gate schedule. It is local and asynchronous. Each bridge computes eligibility times based on local state and configured stream/shaper parameters.

What makes ATS different from CBS?

ATS is normally described as per-stream or per-shaper eligibility-time assignment. CBS shapes a traffic-class queue by credit. ATS computes a timestamp for each arriving frame.

ATS variables

SymbolNameMeaning
tAarrivalTime(frame)Local time when the frame arrives at the ATS processing point.
Llength(frame)Frame length in bits, including any configured media-specific overhead.
rCommittedInformationRateRate at which the virtual token bucket refills, in bits/s.
CCommittedBurstSizeMaximum burst credit, in bits.
EBucketEmptyTimeVirtual timestamp representing the shaper's current credit state.
GGroupEligibilityTimeLatest assigned eligibility time for the shaper group; prevents decreasing eligibility times within the group.
MMaxResidenceTimeMaximum allowed time between frame arrival and assigned eligibility. Used to discard frames that would wait too long.

7. ATS eligibility-time calculation

ATS converts a rate-and-burst contract into a concrete timestamp attached to each frame. The key calculation is not only “do I have tokens now?” Instead, ATS can assign a future eligibility time.

1

Compute how long the frame consumes the configured rate

Length recovery duration
lengthRecoveryDuration = L / r

If a frame consumes L bits and the committed rate is r bits/s, then L/r is the time needed to recover that amount of credit.

2

Compute how long it takes to fill the bucket

Empty-to-full duration
emptyToFullDuration = C / r

This is the maximum useful idle-credit interval. Idle time beyond this does not create unlimited burst credit.

3

Compute the shaper eligibility time

Shaper eligibility time
shaperEligibilityTime = BucketEmptyTime + lengthRecoveryDuration

This asks: based on the current virtual bucket state, when will enough credit exist for this frame?

4

Compute the bucket full time

Bucket full time
bucketFullTime = BucketEmptyTime + emptyToFullDuration

This is the virtual time at which the token bucket would be full. It is used when updating BucketEmptyTime so that idle time beyond the bucket size is not carried forward as infinite credit.

5

Assign the final eligibility time

Final ATS eligibility time
eligibilityTime = max(arrivalTime(frame), GroupEligibilityTime, shaperEligibilityTime)

The frame cannot become eligible before it arrives. It cannot violate the shaper's rate state. It also cannot go backward relative to the shaper group's previous eligibility time.

6

Discard if the frame would wait too long

Residence-time check
if eligibilityTime > arrivalTime(frame) + MaxResidenceTime: discard

This prevents a bad or overloaded stream from building an indefinitely delayed queue.

7

Update ATS state for the next frame

GroupEligibilityTime = eligibilityTime

if eligibilityTime < bucketFullTime:
    BucketEmptyTime = shaperEligibilityTime
else:
    BucketEmptyTime = shaperEligibilityTime + eligibilityTime − bucketFullTime

The first branch is the normal case where the bucket was not full before the frame became eligible. The second branch removes excess idle time beyond the burst-size cap.

8. BucketEmptyTime explained clearly

BucketEmptyTime is the most confusing ATS variable because it sounds like a physical bucket timer. It is better understood as a virtual timestamp that stores the stream's credit state in time units.

Virtual credit intuition
virtualCreditAtTime(t) ≈ (t − BucketEmptyTime) × CommittedInformationRate
E in the past

Positive credit

If current time is after BucketEmptyTime, the stream has accumulated credit. The farther in the past E is, the more credit exists, up to the burst cap.

E equals now

Zero credit

If BucketEmptyTime equals current time, the virtual bucket is empty. A new frame normally needs to wait for L/r.

E in the future

Rate debt

If BucketEmptyTime is in the future, the stream is in debt. A new frame waits until the debt plus its own length has been recovered.

Why the name makes sense

Past BucketEmptyTime E                         Current time t
        |---------------------------------------------|
                  credit accumulated at rate r

credit = (t − E) × r, limited by CommittedBurstSize

If the bucket had zero tokens at E, and tokens refill at rate r, then by time t the bucket would have accumulated (t − E) × r tokens, capped at C.

9. ATS worked examples

9.1 Example 1: frame eligible immediately because credit is available

CommittedInformationRate r = 10 Mbit/s
CommittedBurstSize C       = 12,000 bits
Frame length L             = 4,000 bits
arrivalTime tA             = 10.000 ms
BucketEmptyTime E          = 9.000 ms
GroupEligibilityTime G     = 9.000 ms
lengthRecoveryDuration = L/r
                       = 4000 / 10,000,000
                       = 0.400 ms

emptyToFullDuration    = C/r
                       = 12000 / 10,000,000
                       = 1.200 ms

shaperEligibilityTime  = E + L/r
                       = 9.000 ms + 0.400 ms
                       = 9.400 ms

bucketFullTime         = E + C/r
                       = 9.000 ms + 1.200 ms
                       = 10.200 ms

eligibilityTime        = max(10.000 ms, 9.000 ms, 9.400 ms)
                       = 10.000 ms

The frame is eligible immediately when it arrives. The stream had enough accumulated credit.

9.2 Example 2: burst of four equal frames

Use the same r = 10 Mbit/s, C = 12,000 bits, and L = 4,000 bits. A full bucket can absorb three frames immediately because 3 × 4,000 = 12,000 bits. A fourth frame must wait.

FrameInput state before frameComputed eligibility timeMeaning
1Bucket full at 10.000 ms10.000 msUses first 4000 bits of burst credit.
2Credit remains10.000 msUses second 4000 bits.
3Credit remains10.000 msUses third 4000 bits. Bucket is now empty.
4No burst credit remains10.400 msMust wait for 4000 bits to refill at 10 Mbit/s.
5After frame 410.800 msFrames now spaced by L/r = 400 µs.

9.3 Example 3: frame discarded by MaxResidenceTime

arrivalTime tA        = 10.000 ms
MaxResidenceTime M    = 1.000 ms
BucketEmptyTime E     = 12.000 ms
Frame length L/r      = 0.400 ms
GroupEligibilityTime  = 9.000 ms

shaperEligibilityTime = 12.000 + 0.400 = 12.400 ms
eligibilityTime       = max(10.000, 9.000, 12.400) = 12.400 ms
latest allowed time   = arrivalTime + MaxResidenceTime = 11.000 ms

12.400 ms > 11.000 ms, so the frame is discarded.

This is how ATS prevents an overloaded stream from creating a large hidden backlog.

Very important: ATS eligibility time is not a global ordering of all frames across all traffic classes. It controls when a frame may join local transmission selection. The egress scheduler still decides among currently eligible queues.

11. TAS and IEEE 802.1Qbv from first principles

Time-Aware Shaper (TAS) uses per-queue transmission gates. A closed gate makes a queue unavailable even if frames are waiting and even if the queue would otherwise win strict priority.

Traffic Class 7 gate: OPEN    -> queue may participate
Traffic Class 6 gate: CLOSED  -> queue cannot participate
Traffic Class 5 gate: CLOSED  -> queue cannot participate
Traffic Class 0 gate: OPEN    -> queue may participate

Gate Control List

A Gate Control List (GCL) is a repeating time schedule of gate states. Each entry specifies which traffic-class gates are open or closed and how long that state lasts.

GCL intervalDurationOpen queuesTypical use
Entry 1100 µsTC7 onlyScheduled control traffic
Entry 2300 µsTC5, TC4CBS/ATS shaped traffic
Entry 3600 µsTC0-TC3Best effort and managed background traffic

TAS does not replace the underlying scheduler. If several gates are open at the same time, the port still needs a transmission-selection algorithm. That may be strict priority, CBS-aware priority, ETS, WRR, DRR, or another configured local scheduler.

12. ETS, WRR, and DRR

12.1 Enhanced Transmission Selection

Enhanced Transmission Selection (ETS), from IEEE 802.1Qaz, is a bandwidth-allocation framework for traffic classes. It is not exactly the same as one specific dequeue algorithm. It provides a way to allocate bandwidth among traffic classes while allowing unused bandwidth to be used by other classes.

12.2 Weighted Round Robin

Weighted Round Robin (WRR) visits queues in turns and gives service proportional to configured weights.

Weights:
    Q1 weight = 1
    Q2 weight = 2
    Q3 weight = 3

Simplified service pattern:
    Q1, Q2, Q2, Q3, Q3, Q3, repeat

WRR is simple, but if packet sizes differ, packet-count fairness does not always equal byte fairness.

12.3 Deficit Round Robin

Deficit Round Robin (DRR) adds a quantum and deficit counter per queue. If a queue cannot send because its head-of-line frame is too large for the current deficit, the unused deficit carries forward.

For each queue in each round:
    deficit[queue] += quantum[queue]

    while queue not empty and head_frame_size <= deficit[queue]:
        send head frame
        deficit[queue] -= head_frame_size

    if frame too large:
        keep deficit for next round

DRR is therefore better than naive WRR when frame sizes vary widely.

13. Combining TAS, CBS, ATS, and local schedulers

A realistic TSN-capable switch does not usually choose exactly one mechanism. It layers mechanisms carefully.

Frame belongs to stream S
        |
        v
ATS may assign eligibilityTime to frame
        |
        v
Frame is placed in traffic-class queue
        |
        v
TAS gate for that queue must be open
        |
        v
If the queue uses CBS, credit must be >= 0
        |
        v
Strict Priority / ETS / WRR / DRR chooses among eligible queues
        |
        v
Frame transmitted if enough time remains before any gate-close event

What TAS controls

TAS controls when a queue is allowed to participate. Closed gate means no selection.

What CBS controls

CBS controls whether a shaped traffic-class queue has enough credit to compete.

What ATS controls

ATS controls when each frame becomes eligible based on stream/shaper rate and burst state.

What the scheduler controls

The scheduler controls which eligible queue/frame actually wins the port.

14. Automotive Ethernet perspective

Automotive Ethernet is not judged mainly by average throughput. It is judged by whether critical flows arrive within bounded latency and whether noncritical flows cannot disturb them beyond the engineered budget.

MechanismAutomotive useTypical reason
TASHighly critical scheduled control windowsMinimal latency variation and engineered transmission windows.
CBSAudio/video or periodic rate-constrained streamsSmooths traffic without fully reserving time slots.
ATSMixed event-driven and periodic streams in zonal networksPer-hop reshaping without global TAS schedule everywhere.
Strict PriorityUrgent arbitration among already eligible queuesGives the most critical eligible class first access.
ETS / WRR / DRRManaged residual trafficShares remaining bandwidth among noncritical or less critical classes.

In a vehicle, a good design usually says: use TAS where hard windows are justified, CBS where a class needs smooth bounded service, ATS where per-hop asynchronous reshaping is more practical, strict priority for urgent arbitration, and ETS/weighted service for the managed remainder.

15. Implementation checklist

CBS checklist

  • Map the stream or traffic class to the correct egress queue.
  • Choose idleSlope based on reserved bandwidth.
  • Compute sendSlope = idleSlope − portTransmitRate.
  • Use correct frame-size accounting, including relevant Layer 1 and MAC overhead.
  • Compute hiCredit from maximum interference size.
  • Compute loCredit from maximum shaped frame size.
  • Verify that lower-priority traffic still receives intended service.

ATS checklist

  • Identify the stream or ATS scheduler instance correctly.
  • Configure CommittedInformationRate and CommittedBurstSize.
  • Include configured media-specific overhead in frame length.
  • Maintain BucketEmptyTime per ATS scheduler instance.
  • Maintain GroupEligibilityTime per shaper group.
  • Apply the MaxResidenceTime discard rule.
  • Ensure the egress queue selection logic respects eligibility time.

Common mistakes

MistakeCorrection
“CBS decides final packet order.”No. CBS only controls credit-based eligibility of a shaped queue. The local scheduler still arbitrates.
“ATS creates one universal sorted queue for all streams.”No. ATS assigns eligibility times. Traffic-class mapping and local scheduler rules still matter.
“BucketEmptyTime is a real emptying timer.”No. It is a virtual timestamp representing credit state.
“TAS is the complete scheduler.”No. TAS gates queues. Open queues still use their configured transmission-selection algorithms.
“WRR and ETS are identical.”No. ETS is the standardized bandwidth-allocation framework; WRR is one possible weighted service style.
