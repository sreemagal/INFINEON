Differential Manchester Encoding — The Clock Recovery Problem

Why a self-clocking line code is needed, and how DME solves it

The core problem: In NRZ (Non-Return-to-Zero) encoding, a long run of identical bits produces no transitions on the wire. Without transitions, the receiver has no way to recover the sender's clock and loses synchronisation. Differential Manchester Encoding solves this by guaranteeing a transition at the midpoint of every single bit period — regardless of the data value.
Key insight: The amber dots in the right panel appear at exactly the mid-point of every bit period — in bit 1 periods and bit 0 periods alike. This regularity is what the receiver's clock recovery circuit locks onto. No data pattern can suppress these transitions because the encoding rule mandates them unconditionally.
