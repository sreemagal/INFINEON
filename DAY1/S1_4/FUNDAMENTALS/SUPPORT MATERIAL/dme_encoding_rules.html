Differential Manchester Encoding — The Two Encoding Rules

How each bit value is translated into a waveform pattern within one bit period

DME has exactly two rules applied at every bit boundary. Rule 1 (mandatory mid-bit transition) is unconditional and provides the clock. Rule 2 (optional start-of-period transition) carries the data: present for 0, absent for 1. Both rules together produce a waveform with 1 or 2 transitions per period.

Rule for encoding 0 — two transitions per period

At the start of the bit period: the signal transitions (changes level).
At the mid-point of the bit period: the signal transitions again (mandatory).

The receiver sees two transitions and decodes: bit = 0.

Rule for encoding 1 — one transition per period

At the start of the bit period: no transition. Signal continues unchanged.
At the mid-point of the bit period: the signal transitions (mandatory).

The receiver sees one transition and decodes: bit = 1.

Why "differential"? The bit value is encoded in the presence or absence of a transition at the period boundary — not in the absolute signal level (HIGH = 1, LOW = 0 style). This means if the cable is wired with reversed polarity, the transition pattern is identical and decoding is unaffected. The receiver does not need to know which level is "high" — it only needs to detect transitions.
